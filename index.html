World Power Plug Guide

Different countries use different plug and socket shapes, voltages, and frequencies. This guide helps travelers understand what adapter they need and what the outlet will actually look like.

Quick rule: a plug adapter changes the shape, but a voltage converter changes the electricity level.

How to use this guide

1. Check the destination

Look up the country or region to find its plug type and voltage standard.

2. Check your device label

Many chargers say something like 100-240V, 50/60Hz. If so, you usually only need a plug adapter.

3. Match the outlet

Use the socket visuals below to compare what you expect to see on the wall.

Socket shape details

Type A

Shape: two parallel flat-slot openings.

Common in: United States, Canada, Mexico, Japan.

Type B

Shape: Type A plus a round grounding opening above.

Common in: United States, Canada, Mexico.

Type C

Shape: round recessed area with two round pin holes.

Common in: much of Europe and many small chargers worldwide.

Type D

Shape: three round holes in a compact triangle.

Common in: India, Nepal, Sri Lanka.

Type E

Shape: Type C-like round recess with a central earth pin position above.

Common in: France, Belgium, Poland, Czechia.

Type F

Shape: round recess with side grounding contacts.

Common in: Germany, Spain, Netherlands, much of Europe.

Type G

Shape: three rectangular slots, one above two below.

Common in: United Kingdom, Ireland, Singapore, Malaysia.

Type H

Shape: three-hole Y pattern within a round area.

Common in: Israel and Palestinian territories.

Type I

Shape: two angled flat slots with a vertical earth slot.

Common in: Australia, New Zealand, China, Argentina.

Type J

Shape: compact rounded face with offset earth hole.

Common in: Switzerland and Liechtenstein.

Type K

Shape: rounded face with two lower holes and one upper earth hole.

Common in: Denmark and Greenland.

Type L

Shape: three holes stacked in a straight vertical line.

Common in: Italy, San Marino, Vatican City.

Type M

Shape: larger triangular round-hole layout than Type D.

Common in: South Africa and some high-current contexts.

Type N

Shape: compact rounded three-hole cluster.

Common in: Brazil and South Africa (newer standard variants).

Type O

Shape: three round holes in a neat triangular layout.

Common in: Thailand.

These visuals are simplified educational approximations, designed to help recognition. Real outlet plates can vary by manufacturer and wall hardware style.

Adapter or converter?

Use an adapter when

  • Your device supports dual voltage, usually marked 100-240V
  • You only need the plug to physically fit the wall socket
  • You are charging phones, tablets, laptops, cameras, or USB accessories

Use a converter when

  • Your device only supports one voltage
  • You are using high-power appliances with heating elements or motors
  • The destination voltage is much higher or lower than your appliance rating

Safest travel setup

  • A universal adapter from a reputable brand
  • USB-C charging when possible
  • A quick check of your charger label before you pack
