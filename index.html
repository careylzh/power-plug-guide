World Power Plug Guide

Different countries use different plug shapes, voltages, and frequencies. This guide helps travelers understand what adapter they need and when they also need a voltage converter.

Quick rule: a plug adapter changes the shape, but a voltage converter changes the electricity level.

15 Common plug types are used worldwide, labeled Type A through Type O.
100-127V Common in North America and parts of Japan.
220-240V Common across Europe, Asia, Africa, and Australia.

How to use this guide

1. Check the destination

Look up the country or region to find its plug type and voltage standard.

2. Check your device label

Many chargers say something like 100-240V, 50/60Hz. If so, you usually only need a plug adapter.

3. Be careful with heat devices

Hair dryers, kettles, curling irons, and some older appliances may need a real voltage converter, not just an adapter.

Plug shape details

Type A / B

Shape: two flat parallel blades, with Type B adding a round grounding pin.

Where used: United States, Canada, Mexico, and parts of Japan.

Clicking here helps users picture the familiar North American plug.
Type C / E / F

Shape: two round pins. Type E and F differ mostly in how grounding is handled.

Where used: much of continental Europe.

Useful for showing that many European plugs look similar, but are not identical.
Type G

Shape: three rectangular pins in a triangular layout.

Where used: United Kingdom, Ireland, Singapore, Malaysia, and more.

This one is visually distinctive, so the illustration really helps.
Type I

Shape: two slanted flat pins, often with a vertical grounding pin.

Where used: Australia, New Zealand, China, and Argentina.

The angled blades are the key visual cue here.
Type D / M

Shape: three round pins forming a triangle, with larger versions for higher current.

Where used: India, Nepal, South Africa, and some nearby regions.

Good for showing the difference between round-pin families.
Type J / K / L / N

Shape: regional round-pin variants with different spacing and grounding positions.

Where used: Switzerland, Denmark, Italy, Brazil, and others.

This section helps users understand that not all three-pin round systems are interchangeable.

Adapter or converter?

Use an adapter when

  • Your device supports dual voltage, usually marked 100-240V
  • You only need the plug to physically fit the wall socket
  • You are charging phones, tablets, laptops, cameras, or USB accessories

Use a converter when

  • Your device only supports one voltage
  • You are using high-power appliances with heating elements or motors
  • The destination voltage is much higher or lower than your appliance rating

Safest travel setup

  • A universal adapter from a reputable brand
  • USB-C charging when possible
  • A quick check of your charger label before you pack
